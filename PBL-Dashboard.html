LEAD — OPPORTUNITY MAP

Opportunity Map — where to nudge next

The old cross-language score gap is not the headline. The useful bridge is the pain-points null: complaint-evidence prevalence is effectively language-invariant (+1.7 pp●, p = .564●). That means aspect evidence — not raw cross-language rating gaps — is the more trustworthy, actionable signal. We therefore mine reviewed aspect codes for low-cost nudge opportunities to test next semester. These are exploratory, hypothesis-generating rankings; they do not claim intervention effectiveness.

01 — PROCESSING

From raw text to auditable evidence

Every row carries two parallel signals: a language-specific sentiment score and reviewed keyword evidence. The score gives a comparable category; the evidence gives a human-auditable reason.

  1. Sync & filter. Pull the Hokuriku Google review cache and the Fukui Xiaohongshu guidepost.
  2. Language tagging. Split Google reviews into English-, Japanese-, and Chinese-language rows.
  3. Dual-path scoring. Shared Google star ratings provide the cross-language outcome; reviewed pain-points / enjoyment / recommendation / positive codebooks provide matched evidence. Language-specific sentiment tools remain secondary within-language checks only.
  4. Category rule. A shared threshold maps scores to positive / neutral / negative (positive ≥ 0.05, negative ≤ −0.05); neutral-band width is varied as a robustness check.
  5. Statistical tests. Category-share χ², common-scale star-rating Welch tests, evidence-prevalence tests with multiplicity correction, and a within-POI paired check.
  6. Aggregate-only outputs. Every result is written with denominators, source SHA256, and the generating command; the build fails loud on missing inputs rather than inventing data.
Measurement honesty: VADER, oseti, and SnowNLP scores are not a common scale, so raw scores are never compared across languages. Cross-language comparison uses common Google star ratings and reviewed evidence prevalence. Review rows are nested in POIs, so single-level p-values are descriptive; the within-POI paired test (A2) is the venue-clustering robustness check.
APPENDIX A — MEASUREMENT CHECKS

These checks explain why raw cross-language scores are not the headline. They show a real rating / positivity gap, but also the response-style and measurement caveats that motivate pivoting to reviewed aspect evidence for the opportunity map above.

Decision threshold is α = .05 (two-sided) throughout. Because review rows are nested in POIs, single-level tests are read as descriptive support and the within-POI paired hypothesis is the clustering robustness check. The Chinese-social and within-language analyses shown further down are exploratory/descriptive and are not framed as confirmatory hypotheses.

APPENDIX A — OVERVIEW

Why raw scores are not the centerpiece

English-language reviews consistently give higher ratings and carry more positive experience evidence than Japanese-language reviews of the same Fukui venues, and the gap survives controlling for which venues each group reviews. The cleanest comparison is the shared 1–5 star scale (A1); findings below descend from that cleanest, most interpretable result to the most caveated.

Rival explanation — cross-cultural response style. The consistent EN > JP positivity gap is also consistent with well-documented cross-cultural differences in rating and expressive style: Japanese-language reviewers tend toward more reserved scale use, so the gap may partly reflect expression norms rather than differences in actual experience quality at Fukui venues. This interpretation is reinforced — not refuted — by the pain-points (complaint) null: complaint-evidence prevalence does not differ between groups (+1.7 pp●, p = .564●), meaning the groups differ in how much they praise, not in how much they complain — exactly the asymmetric pattern a positivity-expression difference would produce. The within-POI check (A2) controls for which venues are reviewed but does not rule out this response-style confound.
APPENDIX A1 — RATING CHECK
Strongest · common 1–5 scale

English reviewers give higher star ratings

This is the cleanest cross-language comparison: Google star ratings run on a shared 1–5 scale for both groups, so no tool-specific scoring is involved. English reviews average 4.53● versus 4.26● for Japanese — a difference of +0.27 stars● (95% CI 0.14●–0.40●, p < .001●).

The gap holds when the unit of analysis is tightened from individual reviews to POI averages (+0.27 stars●, p = .035●), and again under the within-POI paired check (A2 below).

APPENDIX A2 — VENUE-CONTROL CHECK
Mixed · venue-controlled

Venue composition is one removed confound — results are partially consistent

Restricting to 14● Fukui POIs reviewed by both groups (from 25● shared candidates, ≥5 reviews per language), the within-POI paired difference in positive-sentiment share is +0.16● (p < .001●), which is significant. The paired difference in star rating is +0.31 stars● (p = .058●), which does not reach significance at .05 (borderline). Holding venue constant removes POI composition as one confound — it does not address reviewer self-selection within a venue, nor the response-style confound described above.

This is a mixed / partial robustness result, not a clean confirmation. The positive-share difference holds within shared venues; the star-rating difference is borderline. Note also that positive-share uses two different scoring tools (VADER for English, oseti for Japanese), so this cross-language comparison is tool-dependent.
APPENDIX A3 — EVIDENCE-PREVALENCE CHECK
Significant · length-confounded

English reviews carry more positive experience evidence — but English texts are longer

English reviews are substantially longer (median 176 chars● vs 62 chars● for Japanese), which means they have more opportunity for any keyword to match; prevalence differences partly reflect text length, not a pure rate. With that caveat noted up front: using reviewed keyword codebooks, English reviews mention enjoyment in 53%● of rows versus 22%● for Japanese — a gap of +31.3 pp● (p < .001●, Benjamini–Hochberg FDR). Positive-sentiment evidence differs by +23.6 pp● (p < .001●) and recommendation evidence by +10.8 pp● (p < .001●).

Pain-points (complaint) evidence is the telling null: the English–Japanese difference is only +1.7 pp● and is not significant (p = .564●). The groups differ in how much they praise, not in how much they complain.
Because English reviews are longer (median 176 chars● vs 62 chars●), more keyword matches are expected by chance alone. Treat prevalence differences as reflecting evidence-rich-text behaviour rather than a pure rate difference.
APPENDIX A4 — SENTIMENT-MIX CHECK
Highly significant · small effect

Sentiment mix differs by review language

The distribution across positive / neutral / negative categories differs significantly between the groups (χ² = 38.6●, p < .001●, Holm-adjusted): 91.1%● of English reviews are positive versus 72.4%● of Japanese. The association is real but small in magnitude — Cramér's V = 0.14● — so it is reported as a significant-but-modest difference, not a dramatic one.

APPENDIX A5 — MECHANISM
Descriptive · within-tool

What moves sentiment inside each language

Within English reviews alone, the presence of enjoyment evidence is the largest driver of higher VADER sentiment (mean-score difference +0.30●, p < .001●, FDR-corrected). Parallel within-Japanese and within-Chinese analyses tell the same story: enjoyment and positive evidence separate sentiment most clearly. These are interpreted within one tool and one language only — never compared as raw scores across groups.

APPENDIX A6 — CHINESE EVIDENCE
Google primary · Xiaohongshu directional

Chinese evidence now starts with star-rated Google reviews

The primary Chinese source is 243● Chinese-language Google reviews across 56● Hokuriku POIs. Their positive signal is the shared Google star rating (mean 4.43 stars●), while reviewed keyword evidence supplies topic and pain-point prevalence. This puts Chinese into the same rating-and-evidence comparison used by the POI opportunity map.

Xiaohongshu remains useful only as a directional guidepost. Keyword evidence converges: 5 of 6● leading topics and 5 of 6● leading pain-point codes are shared across Xiaohongshu and Chinese-language Google reviews, with transport_access ranked first in both. Convergence is about mentioned themes, not sentiment.

SnowNLP is not a Chinese sentiment result. Of 81● rows it labels negative, 64● are high-star reviews (79%●). The unvalidated category shares are therefore not reported; Chinese positives use star ratings.
Pain-point tags mark topic presence, not polarity. They can fire when a high-star review says parking was convenient. Lynn approved the colloquial keyword vocabulary, improving recall, but tagging remains polarity-blind.
POI-level Chinese evidence is directional. Median coverage is 3 reviews/POI●. The language-aware gate blocks a flip when the driving language has fewer than 10 reviews● at that POI or the aspect has fewer than 3 supporting tags●. Thin Chinese evidence can inform follow-up, but cannot drive a headline opportunity classification.
04 — DISCUSSION

What the gap does and does not mean

The defensible headline is the shared-scale star-rating gap (+0.27 stars●, 4.53● vs 4.26●): it is the one measure on a common 1–5 scale, with no tool-specific scoring in between. Its direction is clear; its cause is not. Two readings fit the data equally well — English-language reviewers genuinely experienced Fukui venues more favourably, or English- and Japanese-language reviewers differ in how they express evaluation on a rating scale. This study cannot separate the two.

The most informative result is not the gap itself but the pain-points (complaint) null. Complaint-evidence prevalence is essentially equal across groups (+1.7 pp●, p = .564●): the groups differ in how much they praise, not in how much they complain. A real difference in venue quality would be expected to move complaints too; a difference confined to praise is the asymmetric signature of a positivity-expression difference — i.e. response style — more than of Fukui serving one group worse.

The findings are therefore best read as a hierarchy of evidential strength rather than a single claim. The common-scale star rating is the strongest rung; the sentiment-category mix is weaker (tool-dependent and small in magnitude, Cramér's V = 0.14●); the reviewed-evidence prevalence gap is the most caveated, because English texts are markedly longer (176 chars● vs 62 chars●) and longer text mechanically matches more keywords. Where the evidence is weak, this page says so.

Practical reading for Fukui tourism stakeholders. First, raw cross-language review scores should not be benchmarked directly — a lower Japanese-language average does not by itself mean a worse experience, and a dashboard that ranks venues on mixed-language ratings will be misled by response style. Second, because complaint prevalence is language-independent, pain points evidence — not rating averages — is the more trustworthy place to look for genuine service issues that affect every group.

Limitations

Sample & scope
A Hokuriku convenience cache (Outscraper-derived Google reviews across Fukui, Kanazawa, and Toyama; plus external Chinese-language sources). No claim of national or all-Japan representativeness.
Statistical power
The venue-controlled paired test rests on only 14● shared POIs; the within-POI star-rating difference (+0.31 stars●, p = .058●) is therefore inconclusive, not a demonstrated null — the test is underpowered at this venue overlap.
Measurement
VADER, oseti, and SnowNLP are not validated against hand-labelled Fukui tourism text, and the reviewed keyword codebooks are coverage-limited; both skew positive, so the category test meets a ceiling effect.
Design
Holding venue constant removes POI composition as one confound only; reviewer self-selection within a venue and the response-style confound above remain open. No temporal/trend analysis is attempted, given the date-quality gap.
Chinese strand
Chinese-language Google reviews are primary star-rated evidence; Xiaohongshu is a Fukui-only directional guidepost. Per-POI Chinese coverage remains thin (median 3 reviews/POI●), and keyword pain-point tags remain polarity-blind.

Where this should go next

  1. Calibrate response style. Within-reviewer cross-language pairs or anchoring vignettes to isolate genuine experience differences from scale-use differences — the central unresolved question.
  2. Power up the venue-controlled test. Widen the shared-POI overlap so the borderline within-POI rating result can be resolved rather than left inconclusive.
  3. Validate the scoring tools. Score a hand-labelled tourism subset in each language to quantify how far VADER / oseti / SnowNLP agree with human judgement before leaning on category shares.
  4. Validate Chinese measurement. Hand-label short Chinese reviews for sentiment and pain-point polarity; retain Xiaohongshu as directional context until comparable POI-linked outcomes exist.
  5. Add a temporal view. Once date precision is sufficient, examine whether the language gap is stable or seasonal.
In one line: the cross-language gap is real on the clean scale, but its cause is unresolved — and the equal complaint rate across groups suggests it reflects, at least in part, how groups express evaluation rather than how well Fukui serves them.
