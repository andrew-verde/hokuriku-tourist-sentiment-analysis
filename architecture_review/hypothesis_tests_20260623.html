Recommended Evidence Hierarchy

  1. Primary confirmatory family: JP vs EN Google review comparisons. Same platform, common rating scale, POI metadata, review row unit.
  2. Secondary descriptive family: EN vs JP vs Chinese sentiment category shares and reviewed evidence prevalence. Valid as platformed discourse comparison, not visitor satisfaction comparison.
  3. Exploratory content family: keyword/code/topic rates across languages or source platforms. Use family-wise false discovery control and report precision/recall caveats for codebook matching.
  4. Validation/sensitivity family: POI-cluster bootstrap, POI-level aggregation, neutral-band sensitivity, XHS-only vs combined Chinese variants, library-vs-codebook disagreement.

H1. Sentiment Polarity Distribution: JP vs EN Google Reviews

primary confirmatory same platform

1. Research question

Do English-language and Japanese-language Fukui Google reviews differ in positive/neutral/negative sentiment category prevalence?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Sentiment category distribution is independent of review language group.

H1: Sentiment category distribution differs between English-language and Japanese-language reviews.

3. Variables needed

language_group, sentiment_category, poi_id, poi_category, text_length_chars, optional review_date month.

4. Recommended statistical test

Primary: 2 x 3 chi-square test of independence, with Cramer's V effect size. Use Fisher exact or Monte Carlo exact test if expected counts fall below 5. Sensitivity: multinomial logistic regression with language group as predictor and cluster-robust SE by poi_id.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Check expected cell counts and row denominators.
  • Check POI nesting and imbalance: English n is much smaller than Japanese n.
  • Run neutral-band sensitivity at +/-0.10 and +/-0.20.
  • Inspect text length distribution by language; include length if imbalance is large.

6. Multiple-testing correction

None if preregistered as single primary JP/EN sentiment test. If tested with multiple neutral bands as inferential claims, apply Holm correction across bands.

7. Expected interpretation

A significant result means category shares differ under VADER/oseti threshold rules. Do not interpret as raw sentiment score equivalence or nationality difference.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

VADER and oseti are different tools. Review rows are nested within POIs. Language groups may visit different POI categories and write different length reviews.

H2. Common-Scale Star Rating Difference: JP vs EN Google Reviews

primary companion outcome common measurement scale

1. Research question

Do English-language and Japanese-language Fukui Google reviews differ in Google star ratings?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Mean review rating is equal across English-language and Japanese-language reviews.

H1: Mean review rating differs across language groups.

3. Variables needed

language_group, review_rating, poi_id, poi_category, review_date.

4. Recommended statistical test

Welch t-test on review rows. Sensitivity: POI-level Welch t-test on POI-language mean ratings; ordinal logistic model if distribution is strongly ceiling-bound; cluster bootstrap by POI.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Rating scale is common 1-to-5 Google outcome.
  • Check rating distribution, ceiling effects, variance inequality, and POI category mix.
  • Check whether English reviews cover fewer POIs or different POI categories.

6. Multiple-testing correction

None if this is declared companion outcome. If paired with many category or POI subgroup rating tests, apply Benjamini-Hochberg FDR within rating family.

7. Expected interpretation

Difference estimates are interpretable because both groups use same star scale. This validates or contextualizes text sentiment; it does not replace text analysis.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Ratings are not pure sentiment text measures. POI selection, date window, review culture, and platform behavior may drive differences.

H3. Reviewed Evidence Prevalence: Friction, Enjoyment, Recommendation

primary content evidence multiple binary outcomes

1. Research question

Do JP and EN Google reviews differ in prevalence of reviewed keyword evidence for friction, enjoyment, recommendation, and positive sentiment?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Reviewed evidence prevalence for each evidence family is equal across JP and EN reviews.

H1: At least one evidence family has different prevalence by language group.

3. Variables needed

any_friction, any_enjoyment_evidence, any_recommendation_evidence, any_positive_evidence, language_group, text_length_chars, poi_id, poi_category.

4. Recommended statistical test

For each binary evidence family: two-proportion test or Fisher exact test, plus odds ratio. Preferred model: logistic regression evidence_present ~ language_group + text_length_chars + poi_category with cluster-robust SE by poi_id.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Reviewed codebook matching precision should be sampled manually before strong claims.
  • Check sparse evidence cells and separation in logistic models.
  • Check text length because longer reviews have more chance to contain any keyword.
  • Check language-specific codebook balance: number of reviewed terms and semantic coverage by family.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Use Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across evidence families. If testing many individual codes inside each family, apply FDR within family and report family as exploratory.

7. Expected interpretation

Evidence prevalence differences support claims about what review language groups mention, not direct psychological satisfaction.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Substring keyword matching can miss synonyms and can produce false positives. Translation equivalence across Japanese and English terms is not guaranteed.

H4. Cross-Source Sentiment Category Shares: EN, JP, Chinese

secondary descriptive cross-platform caveat

1. Research question

Do sentiment category shares differ across English-language Google reviews, Japanese-language Google reviews, and Chinese-language social rows?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Positive/neutral/negative category shares are equal across language/source groups.

H1: At least one language/source group has different category shares.

3. Variables needed

Group label, sentiment_category, source variant label, denominator, and row unit. For Chinese rows: source_platform, theme, body_has_text, post_date_precision.

4. Recommended statistical test

3 x 3 chi-square test for category independence with Cramer's V. Pairwise chi-square or Fisher tests only after global test, with FDR correction. Main Chinese variant should be preregistered: non-fan XHS-only for cleaner source comparison, combined Chinese as sensitivity.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Confirm denominators and source variant before testing.
  • Check expected counts, especially neutral Chinese cells.
  • Report row-unit mismatch: review row vs note row vs comment row.
  • Check Chinese source mix: XHS and Douyin should be stratified, not silently pooled.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Global test first. Pairwise group tests use Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across three pairwise comparisons.

7. Expected interpretation

Result describes platformed sentiment category distributions. It should be phrased as discourse category difference, not satisfaction ranking.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Tools differ by language. Source platforms differ. Chinese social rows may include fandom, comments, and inferred dates. Raw score comparisons are invalid.

H5. Positive And Negative Sentiment Prevalence

secondary binary reductions

1. Research question

Are positive-vs-not-positive and negative-vs-not-negative prevalence different across language/source groups?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Binary sentiment prevalence is equal across groups.

H1: Binary sentiment prevalence differs across groups.

3. Variables needed

sentiment_category recoded to positive_vs_not and negative_vs_not, language/source group, text length, POI or platform fields.

4. Recommended statistical test

Chi-square/Fisher on binary contingency tables. For JP/EN: logistic regression with cluster-robust SE by POI. For EN/JP/CN: descriptive logistic regression with group indicator and text length, reported with source caveats.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Binary reduction discards neutral category information; compare with H1/H4 category results.
  • Check event counts for negative class; sparse cells may need Fisher exact or penalized logistic regression.
  • Run neutral threshold sensitivity.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Positive and negative binary tests form one sentiment-prevalence family. Apply Holm correction if both are confirmatory; FDR if exploratory pairwise tests are added.

7. Expected interpretation

Useful for slide-friendly prevalence statements, but category-share results are more complete and should remain primary.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Positive evidence can be easier to detect than negative evidence in tourism text. Negative comments may be shorter, rarer, or platform-suppressed.

H6. Keyword Usage Differences By Reviewed Code Family

exploratory content reviewed evidence

1. Research question

Which reviewed friction, enjoyment, recommendation, and sentiment evidence codes differ most across language/source groups?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Each code's occurrence prevalence is equal across compared groups.

H1: One or more codes have unequal occurrence prevalence across groups.

3. Variables needed

Per-code binary columns or matched-term fields for JP/EN and Chinese, denominator rows, language/source group, text length, POI/platform covariates.

4. Recommended statistical test

For each code: Fisher exact test or logistic regression. Report odds ratios, risk differences, and Wilson confidence intervals. For all codes in a family: global chi-square screen followed by per-code FDR-controlled tests.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Use code families, not literal keywords, for cross-script comparison.
  • Audit translation/equivalence: a Japanese term and English term may not express same intensity.
  • Flag codes with low occurrence and suppress unstable rates below minimum n.
  • Check multi-match inflation: one row can match several terms in same family.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Mandatory. Use Benjamini-Hochberg FDR within each code family. For high-stakes confirmatory subset, use Holm.

7. Expected interpretation

Results identify content themes more salient in one language/source group. Use effect sizes and confidence intervals, not p-values alone.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Codebook coverage may differ by language. Substring matches do not equal human-coded intent. Terms may reflect POI mix or source prompt rather than visitor concern.

H7. Topic Occurrence Rates Across Language/Source Groups

exploratory needs aligned topic contract

1. Research question

Do language/source groups emphasize different tourism topics such as history/culture, temples/spiritual sites, scenic nature, food, hot springs, dinosaurs/museums, crafts/shopping, accommodation, and seasonal events?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Topic occurrence prevalence is equal across language/source groups for each aligned topic.

H1: At least one aligned topic has different occurrence prevalence by language/source group.

3. Variables needed

Chinese topic booleans already exist. For JP/EN, either reviewed topic booleans must be added, or poi_category must be explicitly labeled as destination-type proxy, not text topic.

4. Recommended statistical test

Preferred future test: per-topic logistic regression topic_present ~ group + text_length_chars with cluster/source sensitivity. Current feasible proxy test: compare Google poi_category mix by language group with chi-square; compare Chinese topic distributions separately, not as direct cross-language topic equality.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Aligned topic codebook must be symmetrical across languages before cross-language topic claims.
  • POI category is not equivalent to what review text mentions.
  • Chinese rows can mention multiple topics; Google review POI category is single metadata label.
  • Fan-pilgrimage topic requires separate reporting because it is source-specific.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Use FDR across topic families. Predefine top topics to avoid post hoc cherry-picking.

7. Expected interpretation

With aligned topic codebook, results support claims about topical emphasis. With current POI-category proxy only, results support destination mix diagnostics, not textual topic prevalence.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Main threat is construct mismatch: topic mention, POI category, travel motive, and platform hashtag are different constructs.

H8. Cross-Source Friction And Enjoyment Evidence Prevalence

secondary descriptive reviewed evidence path

1. Research question

Do EN Google reviews, JP Google reviews, and Chinese-language XHS rows differ in prevalence of friction and enjoyment/recommendation evidence?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Evidence prevalence is equal across language/source groups.

H1: Evidence prevalence differs for at least one language/source group.

3. Variables needed

any_friction, any_enjoyment_evidence, language/source group, chosen Chinese source variant, text length, POI/platform fields.

4. Recommended statistical test

3-group chi-square test for each binary outcome. Pairwise Fisher tests with FDR. Sensitivity: JP/EN-only same-platform logistic model and Chinese XHS-only vs combined source comparison.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Confirm reviewed JP/EN codebook config and Chinese runtime codebook are active.
  • Check source variant: XHS-only should be main cross-source comparison; Douyin combined output is sensitivity.
  • Check row-unit mismatch and text length.

6. Multiple-testing correction

FDR across friction and enjoyment/recommendation binary outcomes. If drilling into individual friction codes, apply FDR within friction-code family.

7. Expected interpretation

Differences indicate source-specific discourse emphasis. Example: XHS may surface itinerary/access planning concerns more than Google reviews because post intent differs.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Cross-source rows are not exchangeable. XHS notes, Douyin comments, and Google reviews have different prompts, audiences, and lengths.

H9. Within-Chinese Platform Effects: Xiaohongshu vs Douyin

source-sensitivity important confound check

1. Research question

Do Xiaohongshu rows and Douyin comment rows differ in sentiment category, friction evidence, enjoyment evidence, and topic rates?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Outcome prevalence/distribution is equal across Chinese source platforms.

H1: Outcome prevalence/distribution differs between source platforms.

3. Variables needed

source_platform, sentiment_category, any_friction, any_enjoyment_evidence, topic booleans, theme, text_length_chars, body/text scope fields.

4. Recommended statistical test

Chi-square/Fisher for categorical outcomes; Mann-Whitney for text length; logistic regression for binary evidence with text length and theme controls if sample size supports it.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Check source denominator balance: current combined output is dominated by Douyin comments.
  • Check whether Douyin comments lack theme annotations or have local parser IDs only.
  • Suppress rates for small source-theme slices below n=10.

6. Multiple-testing correction

FDR across tested Chinese outcomes. Separate source-platform diagnostics from cross-language claims.

7. Expected interpretation

If platform effects are large, cross-language comparisons using pooled Chinese rows are unstable. Use XHS-only or stratified Chinese outputs in main analysis.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Platform effect is confounded with text type: note vs comment. Douyin timestamps are relative/inferred and comments may be shorter.

H10. Topic-Sentiment Association

exploratory content interpretation

1. Research question

Are certain topics or POI categories associated with positive or negative sentiment, and does that association differ by language/source group?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Topic/category presence is not associated with sentiment category after accounting for group.

H1: Topic/category presence is associated with sentiment, or association differs by group.

3. Variables needed

Sentiment category or positive/negative binary, topic booleans for Chinese, poi_category for Google, future JP/EN topic booleans for aligned test, group, text length, source platform.

4. Recommended statistical test

Within Chinese: logistic regression positive_vs_not ~ topic + source_platform + text_length_chars. Within JP/EN: logistic or multinomial model sentiment ~ language_group + poi_category + language_group:poi_category with POI clustering. Cross-language aligned-topic version only after JP/EN topic codebook exists.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Check sparse topic cells and multiple topics per row.
  • Check multicollinearity between topic and source platform/theme.
  • Use marginal effects, not only coefficients.

6. Multiple-testing correction

FDR across topic terms and interaction tests. Treat interactions as exploratory unless preregistered.

7. Expected interpretation

Can show which content domains carry positive or negative discourse. Strongest as within-source analysis; weaker across source types.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Topic-sentiment association is not causal. Some topics may appear because of popular POIs or campaigns, not because topic causes sentiment.

H11. POI Category Mix: JP vs EN Google Reviews

primary diagnostic same platform

1. Research question

Do English-language and Japanese-language Google reviews cover different types of Fukui POIs?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: POI category distribution is independent of review language group.

H1: POI category distribution differs by review language group.

3. Variables needed

language_group, poi_category, poi_id, review count per POI-language group.

4. Recommended statistical test

Chi-square test on language_group x poi_category. Use Fisher/Monte Carlo if sparse. Complement with standardized residuals and category share differences.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Check category counts and rare categories; combine only with documented rationale.
  • Inspect whether few high-volume POIs dominate one language group.
  • Use POI-level counts as sensitivity, not only review rows.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Global chi-square has no correction. If examining individual category residuals as tests, apply FDR across categories.

7. Expected interpretation

If POI mix differs, sentiment and keyword differences may partly reflect destination mix. Use as diagnostic and possible covariate, not headline outcome.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

POI metadata categories are analysis labels, not necessarily tourist motivations. Reviews are nested within POIs.

H12. Text Length And Evidence Detection Bias

diagnostic bias control

1. Research question

Do language/source groups differ in text length, and does text length affect probability of keyword evidence detection?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Text length distribution and evidence detection probability are not associated with language/source group.

H1: Text length differs by group and/or predicts evidence detection.

3. Variables needed

text_length_chars, group label, all binary evidence outcomes, source platform, POI ID/category where available.

4. Recommended statistical test

Kruskal-Wallis for text length across three groups; Mann-Whitney for JP/EN. Logistic regression any_evidence ~ text_length_chars + group for detection bias.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Character counts differ by writing system; consider within-language length controls and log length.
  • Inspect extreme lengths and title-only/body-only status for Chinese rows.
  • Check whether including length changes group effect estimates.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Use as diagnostic. If many evidence outcomes are tested with length, FDR across outcomes.

7. Expected interpretation

Length differences warn that keyword prevalence may partly reflect opportunity to match terms. Adjusted and unadjusted estimates should be shown together.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Character count is not equal semantic length across Chinese, Japanese, and English. Tokenization would improve diagnostics but adds language-specific choices.

H13. Temporal Coverage And Seasonality Diagnostics

diagnostic Chinese dates weak

1. Research question

Do review date coverage and season/month distribution differ between JP and EN Google reviews, and could this confound sentiment/topic comparisons?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Date missingness and month/season distribution are independent of language group.

H1: Date missingness or temporal distribution differs by language group.

3. Variables needed

language_group, parseable review_date, month/season, date missing indicator, sentiment category.

4. Recommended statistical test

Chi-square for date missingness and month/season distribution. Optional Kolmogorov-Smirnov test for date distribution among parseable dates. Add month/season as covariate in JP/EN sensitivity models.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Check date parseability by group. Current aggregate shows EN has missing dates while JP has full date coverage.
  • Do not include Chinese monthly trends until exact date scrub requirements are met.
  • Report date range and missing counts in every sentiment table.

6. Multiple-testing correction

Diagnostic only. If month-specific outcome tests are run, use FDR across months or treat as seasonal model with one global test.

7. Expected interpretation

Temporal imbalance may explain sentiment differences if one group overrepresents peak seasons, events, or recent reviews.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Collection window and Google sorting may influence observed dates. Chinese dates are often inferred and should not be mixed into confirmatory monthly tests.

H14. Library Sentiment vs Reviewed Positive Evidence Disagreement

validation audit trail

1. Research question

Does disagreement between library sentiment category and reviewed positive evidence differ by language group?

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

H0: Disagreement prevalence is equal across language groups.

H1: Disagreement prevalence differs by language group.

3. Variables needed

sentiment_category, any_positive_evidence, library_vs_reviewed_positive_disagreement or computable equivalent, language group, text length, POI ID/category.

4. Recommended statistical test

Two-proportion/Fisher test for JP vs EN disagreement rates. Logistic regression with language and text length, cluster-robust by POI. For Chinese, run separately because SnowNLP domain mismatch is different.

5. Assumptions and diagnostics

  • Define disagreement rule before testing, for example library positive category vs absence of reviewed positive/recommendation evidence.
  • Sample rows manually from each disagreement cell for audit, without committing row text.
  • Check if disagreement concentrated in sarcasm, mixed reviews, or short texts.

6. Multiple-testing correction

FDR if multiple disagreement definitions are tested. Prefer one preregistered definition plus qualitative audit notes.

7. Expected interpretation

High or asymmetric disagreement warns against treating library sentiment as primary evidence. It can guide codebook refinement.

8. Limitations and threats to validity

Absence of keyword evidence is not absence of sentiment. Library tools and codebooks capture different constructs.

Pre-Analysis Checklist

  1. Freeze input variant: JP/EN Fukui Google rows, Chinese XHS-only non-fan, Chinese combined sensitivity, or another named subset.
  2. Reconcile Chinese denominators across aggregate outputs before running final tests.
  3. Record source paths, SHA256 hashes, generated time, commands, filters, and excluded rows.
  4. Keep row-level text, authors, URLs, handles, source IDs, and screenshots out of committed outputs.
  5. Define primary hypotheses before running exploratory per-code/topic scans.
  6. Use cluster-aware sensitivity for Google reviews because rows nest in POIs.
  7. Use exact/Fisher tests for sparse cells and suppress unstable small-slice rates.
  8. Report effect sizes: Cramer's V, odds ratios, risk differences, confidence intervals, and standardized residuals.
  9. Run sensitivity: POI-level aggregation, neutral-band thresholds, XHS-only vs combined Chinese, with/without fan theme.
  10. Write claims as language/source discourse differences, not nationality or intrinsic satisfaction differences.
